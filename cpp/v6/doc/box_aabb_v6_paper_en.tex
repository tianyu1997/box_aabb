\begin{abstract}
We present \textsc{SafeBoxForest}~(SBF), a two-phase motion-planning
framework for high-DOF serial manipulators.
Offline, SBF covers configuration space with \emph{certified safe boxes}
(axis-aligned hyper-rectangles proven collision-free) using a Lifelong
Envelope Cache Tree (LECT) with dual-channel link envelopes and
interchangeable sources (interval FK, boundary sampling, analytical
solver, and geometric critical-point cache).
A coordinated multi-tree growth strategy with inline midpoint bridging
achieves inter-tree connectivity without a separate bridging stage,
producing a navigable box forest in ${\sim}2.3$\,s median
(4{,}187--5{,}025 boxes).
Online, SBF locates start/goal boxes, performs A* graph search with a
tiered proxy mechanism, and improves the path through multi-trial RRT,
elastic-band smoothing, and multi-layer safety fallbacks.
On a 7-DOF KUKA IIWA14 in shelf-barrier scenes, SBF achieves 100\%
success, path lengths of 1.908--5.169\,rad, and query times of
0.024--0.468\,s.
The system employs a modular 10-module/42-header architecture
with good compilation locality.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Motion planning, configuration space, safe box, interval arithmetic,
envelope caching, parallel forest, path optimization, serial manipulator.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Sampling-based motion planning~\cite{lavalle2006planning,lavalle1998rapidly,%
kavraki1996probabilistic} remains the dominant approach for
high-DOF manipulators, yet online latency and path quality still
depend strongly on collision-checking cost and sampling strategy.
Graph-of-Convex-Sets~(GCS)~\cite{marcucci2024motion} formulates motion
planning as a shortest-path problem over convex regions, but requires
expensive convex decomposition of free space
(e.g., IRIS~\cite{deits2015computing}, IRIS-NP~\cite{werner2024fast}).

SafeBoxForest (SBF) takes a different approach: it covers $\Cfree$ with
axis-aligned hyper-rectangles (boxes) that admit $O(D)$ adjacency tests
and $O(1)$ containment queries, enabling fast graph construction and
query-time planning.

\textbf{Main contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item A \emph{dual-channel LECT} with four interchangeable envelope
    sources ($4{\times}3$ source$\times$envelope pipeline), Z4
    rotational symmetry acceleration, and incremental FK optimization;
  \item A \emph{five-phase analytical solver} with DH-chain direct
    coefficient extraction ($37\%$ speedup) and certified BnB verifier
    with monotonicity reduction ($\geq 98\%$ fast path);
  \item A \emph{Sparse Voxel BitMask} with turbo scanline rasterization
    ($42\%$ tighter envelopes, zero-loss merge);
  \item A \emph{coordinated multi-tree growth} strategy with inline
    midpoint bridging, eliminating the separate RRT bridging phase;
  \item A \emph{multi-layer query pipeline} with multi-trial RRT
    competition, elastic-band optimization, and three-tier safety
    fallbacks;
  \item A \emph{modular architecture} with 10~modules / 42~headers
    and improved compilation locality;
  \item Validation on KUKA IIWA14 (7-DOF): $2.3$\,s median build,
    100\% success, 1.908--5.169\,rad paths, 0.024--0.468\,s queries.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Workspace Envelopes and Collision Detection}

Traditional AABB computation relies on dense sampling or interval
arithmetic~\cite{moore2009introduction,jaulin2001applied,merlet2004solving,%
merlet2006interval}.
FCL~\cite{pan2012fcl} and OBB-trees~\cite{gottschalk1996obbtree} handle
single-configuration collision but not interval ranges.
GJK-based methods~\cite{gilbert1988fast} compute distances between
convex shapes efficiently but require per-query evaluation.
Broad-phase collision detection using spatial hashing~\cite{teschner2003optimized}
and bounding volume hierarchies (BVH)~\cite{ericson2004real} reduce
the number of narrow-phase checks.
SBF's LECT amortises interval-level collision refutation across a
persistent KD-tree with dual-channel caching, effectively serving as
a configuration-space BVH that persists across planning queries.

\subsection{Configuration Space Decomposition}

Decomposing C-space into collision-free convex regions enables
convex-optimization-based planning.
IRIS~\cite{deits2015computing} inflates ellipsoids in workspace
via semidefinite programming;
IRIS-NP~\cite{werner2024fast} extends this to C-space using
nonlinear programming.
Recent advances include IRIS-ZO~\cite{werner2024irisze}, which
eliminates the need for gradient information, and
clique cover methods~\cite{amice2024clique} that find
maximal collections of overlapping convex sets.
Werner~\etal~\cite{werner2024vcd} further study convex decomposition
for manipulation.
SBF differs fundamentally: it uses axis-aligned hyper-rectangles (boxes)
rather than general polytopes, exploiting the structure for
$O(D)$ adjacency tests, $O(1)$ containment queries, and persistent
KD-tree caching.
While polytopes may capture free space more tightly per region,
SBF compensates with orders-of-magnitude faster region generation
(milliseconds vs.\ seconds per region for IRIS-NP).

\subsection{Convex-Set-Based Motion Planning}

GCS~\cite{marcucci2024motion} formulates motion planning as a
shortest-path problem on a graph of convex sets, obtaining globally
optimal trajectories via convex relaxation.
The GCS framework requires pre-computed convex regions as input,
typically from IRIS-NP~\cite{werner2024fast}.
SBF's box forest maps directly to a GCS graph:
each box becomes a vertex, and adjacency edges carry overlap
information.
This positions SBF as a potential fast front-end for GCS,
replacing the computationally expensive IRIS-NP decomposition
(see \Cref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Sampling-Based Motion Planning}

RRT~\cite{lavalle1998rapidly} and RRT-Connect~\cite{kuffner2000rrt}
are the de facto standards for single-query planning.
Asymptotically optimal variants
(RRT*~\cite{karaman2011sampling},
Informed-RRT*~\cite{gammell2014informed},
BIT*~\cite{gammell2020batch})
converge to optimal solutions given sufficient time.
Parallel RRT research~\cite{bialkowski2011massively} targets GPU
parallelism for tree expansion.
SBF's coordinated multi-tree strategy differs from both:
it grows trees in box-space (not point-space), using LECT
snapshots for spatial isolation, with incremental UnionFind for
real-time connectivity tracking.

\subsection{Trajectory Optimization}

CHOMP~\cite{ratliff2009chomp} and TrajOpt~\cite{schulman2014motion}
optimize trajectories using covariant gradient descent and
sequential quadratic programming, respectively.
KOMO~\cite{toussaint2017newton} frames motion planning as
$k$-order Markov optimization.
These methods require good initial guesses to avoid local minima
in non-convex environments.
SBF's multi-layer path pipeline (A* search followed by elastic-band
smoothing and greedy shortcut) provides a globally connected
initial path that could serve as a warm start for trajectory
optimizers.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Configuration Space Formalisation}

\begin{definition}[Interval Box]
For a $D$-DOF revolute robot, an \emph{interval box} is
$\mathcal{B} = \prod_{i=1}^{D}[l_i, u_i] \subset \Real^D$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Free Space]
Given obstacle set $\calO = \{O_j\}$ (workspace AABBs),
$\Cfree = \{q \in \Real^D \mid \forall k,\;\mathrm{Link}_k(q) \cap
\calO = \emptyset\}$.
\end{definition}

A box $\mathcal{B}$ is \emph{safe} iff $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \Cfree$.
\Cref{fig:cspace} illustrates the box-forest concept in a 2-DOF C-space.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig2_cspace_boxes.png}
\caption{Conceptual 2-DOF C-space box forest.  Collision obstacles
(gray circles) partition the C-space; collision-free boxes (colored by
tree ID) are refined via recursive splitting near obstacle boundaries.
The A* path (red) traverses adjacent boxes from start to goal.}
\label{fig:cspace}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Modified DH Convention}

Using the Modified DH (Craig) convention~\cite{craig2005introduction},
link $k$'s FK is
$T_k^0(\mathbf{q}) = \prod_{i=1}^{k} A_i(q_i)$,
with each link modelled as a line segment
$\mathbf{p}_{k-1}(\mathbf{q})$ to $\mathbf{p}_k(\mathbf{q})$.

\subsection{Link Envelope and Collision Refutation}

\begin{definition}[Link iAABB]\label{def:iaabb}
For link $k$ and box $\mathcal{B}$:
\begin{equation}
\iAABB_k(\mathcal{B}) = \prod_{d \in \{x,y,z\}}
\left[\min_{q \in \mathcal{B}} p_k^d(q),\;
      \max_{q \in \mathcal{B}} p_k^d(q)\right].
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Collision Refutation]
If $\iAABB_k(\mathcal{B}) \cap O_j = \emptyset$, then link $k$
does not collide with $O_j$ for any $q \in \mathcal{B}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{def:iaabb}, $\iAABB_k(\mathcal{B})$ is the smallest
axis-aligned bounding box containing every position $p_k(q)$
for $q \in \mathcal{B}$.  In particular, for every
$q \in \mathcal{B}$, $p_k(q) \in \iAABB_k(\mathcal{B})$.
Since $\iAABB_k(\mathcal{B}) \cap O_j = \emptyset$ and $O_j$ is an
AABB, no point of $\iAABB_k(\mathcal{B})$ lies in $O_j$.
Hence $p_k(q) \notin O_j$ for every $q \in \mathcal{B}$,
which implies link $k$ does not collide with obstacle $O_j$ at
any configuration in $\mathcal{B}$.
\end{proof}

\section{Certified Envelope Computation}\label{sec:envelope}

SBF provides four endpoint sources with increasing tightness:

\subsection{Interval FK (IFK)}\label{sec:ifk}

Replaces scalar operations in the FK chain with interval arithmetic,
yielding a conservative superset of the true reachable set.
Cost: $O(D)$ interval matrix multiplications.

\subsection{Boundary Critical Sampling (CritSample)}\label{sec:critsample}

For each joint $j$, the critical angle set is
$C_j = \{q_j^-, q_j^+\} \cup \{k\pi/2 : k\pi/2 \in (q_j^-, q_j^+)\}$.
All DH transforms $\bm{T}_j(c)$ for $(j, c \in C_j)$ are precomputed,
eliminating $\sin/\cos$ from the hot path.
CritSample captures 99.4\% of analytical volume at ${\sim}39\,\mu$s
(vs.\ ${\sim}7\,$ms for GCPC).

\subsection{Analytical Critical-Point Solver}\label{sec:analytical}

A five-phase solver enumerates candidate extremisers:

\emph{Phase 0} --- Vertex enumeration ($|F|=0$).
\emph{Phase 1} --- Edge critical points ($|F|=1$) via direct 1D DH
coefficient extraction + sinusoidal solve.
\emph{Phase 2} --- Face critical points ($|F|=2$) via direct 2D DH
extraction + degree-$\leq 8$ companion-matrix solve.
\emph{Phase 2.5} --- Pair-constrained critical points ($q_i + q_j = k\pi/2$).
\emph{Phase 3} --- Interior coordinate descent ($|F|\geq 3$),
with dual-Phase-3 fusion for basin-shift robustness.

\begin{theorem}[Face-$k$ completeness for $k \leq 2$]\label{thm:face}
If the global extremiser has free set $|F|\leq 2$, the solver finds
a configuration with equal or better objective.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $f(q) = p_{\ell,d}(q)$ denote coordinate $d$ of the endpoint
of link $\ell$ as a function of the joint vector $q \in \mathcal{B}$.
The DH chain gives $f(q) = e_d^\top T_1(q_1) \cdots T_D(q_D)\,b$
where each $T_i(q_i)$ is a $4{\times}4$ DH transform.

\emph{Case $|F|=0$ (vertices).}
All joints are at their box boundaries.
There are exactly $2^D$ vertices; Phase~0 evaluates all of them.
Hence the extremiser is found.

\emph{Case $|F|=1$ (edges).}
Fix all joints except $q_j$ at their boundary values.
Then $f_j(\theta) = a\cos\theta + b\sin\theta + c$
for coefficients $a, b, c$ extractable from the DH chain.
By direct differentiation, $f'_j(\theta) = 0$ yields
$\tan\theta = b/a$, giving at most 2 critical points in
$[q_j^-, q_j^+]$ plus the 2 endpoints.
Phase~1 solves this exactly via DH coefficient extraction.

\emph{Case $|F|=2$ (faces).}
Fix all joints except $q_i, q_j$ at boundary values.
Expanding the DH product, $f$ is a trigonometric polynomial in
$(q_i, q_j)$ of the form
$f = \sum_{m,n} \alpha_{mn} \cos^m(q_i)\sin^n(q_i) \cos^p(q_j) \sin^r(q_j)$.
Setting $\partial f/\partial q_i = 0$ and
$\partial f/\partial q_j = 0$ and substituting
$c_i = \cos q_i$, $s_i = \sin q_i$ (with $c_i^2 + s_i^2 = 1$)
yields a polynomial system solvable by companion-matrix
eigendecomposition of degree $\leq 8$.
Phase~2 enumerates all real roots within the box; hence
every interior critical point on a 2-face is found.

Since each case exhaustively enumerates all critical points on
the respective face, any global extremiser with $|F| \leq 2$
is discovered by the combined Phase~0/1/2 enumeration.
\end{proof}

DH-chain direct coefficient extraction replaces QR fitting,
reducing Phase 1+2 from 22.9\,ms to 14.4\,ms ($37\%$ speedup).

\subsubsection{Interval-FK Certification}
Clamping guarantees
$\widehat{\iAABB}_\ell \supseteq \iAABB^{\mathrm{true}}_\ell$
regardless of Phase 3 completeness.

\subsection{Geometric Critical-Point Cache (GCPC)}\label{sec:gcpc}

An eight-phase pipeline that amortises the analytical solver via KD-tree
cache lookup, boundary enumeration, 1D/2D direct DH solves,
pair-constrained modes, and interior coordinate descent.
Symmetry reductions ($q_0$ elimination, $q_1$ period-$\pi$) halve
storage.
Produces endpoint-iAABBs at $0.89{\times}$ the analytical solver's
cost with near-identical volumes.

\subsection{Certified BnB Verifier with Monotonicity Reduction}\label{sec:bnb}

For $|F|\geq 3$, a Krawczyk interval-Newton verifier with
monotonicity-based dimension reduction closes the Phase 3
completeness gap.

\begin{theorem}[Monotone corner optimality]\label{thm:monotone}
If $p_{\ell,d}$ is strictly monotone in every free dimension,
the global extremum is at the corner determined by gradient signs.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $f(q) = p_{\ell,d}(q)$ and suppose $\partial f / \partial q_i$
has constant sign on $\mathcal{B}$ for every free dimension
$i \in F$.
Without loss of generality, consider maximisation.
For each $i \in F$, if $\partial f / \partial q_i > 0$ on
$\mathcal{B}$, then $f$ is strictly increasing in $q_i$ and
the maximum is attained at $q_i = q_i^+$ (the upper bound).
Conversely, if $\partial f / \partial q_i < 0$, the maximum is
at $q_i = q_i^-$.
Since the box $\mathcal{B}$ is a Cartesian product, fixing
each $q_i$ to its optimal boundary value independently yields
a corner point $q^* \in \mathrm{corners}(\mathcal{B})$ with
$f(q^*) \geq f(q)$ for all $q \in \mathcal{B}$.
The argument for minimization is symmetric.

\emph{Probability estimate.}
For a DH chain with $n$ joints and box half-width $w$,
the probability that all dimensions are monotone grows as
$w \to 0$.  At $w = 0.005\,$rad and $n = 7$,
empirical measurement yields monotonicity in $>98.9\%$ of all
box--link--coordinate instances, reducing the BnB verifier
to two scalar FK evaluations per dimension.
\end{proof}

\section{Sparse Voxel BitMask}\label{sec:voxel}

\subsection{BitBrick Layout}

The workspace is tiled into $8^3 = 512$-voxel BitBricks
(64~bytes, one cache line), addressed by spatial hashing (FNV-1a).

\subsection{16-Point Convex Hull Rasterization}

Given proximal box $B_1$ and distal box $B_2$, the swept volume is
$S_\ell = \mathrm{Conv}(\mathrm{corners}(B_1) \cup \mathrm{corners}(B_2))$
with at most 16 vertices.
A turbo scanline algorithm eliminates per-voxel distance evaluations
($O(n_y \cdot n_z)$ complexity, $O(1)$ per scanline).

\subsection{Voxel Operations}

\emph{Collision}: 64-bit AND per word with early exit.
\emph{Merge}: bitwise OR preserves exact shape (zero volume inflation).

\section{LECT: Lifelong Envelope Cache Tree}\label{sec:lect}

\subsection{Architecture}

The LECT is a lazy KD-tree over C-space.
Each node $v$ stores a box $B_v$ and caches link envelopes on demand:
\begin{equation}
  \iAABB_\ell(B_v) = \mathrm{bbox}\bigl([\bm{p}_{\ell-1}]_{B_v},
  [\bm{p}_\ell]_{B_v}\bigr) \oplus r_\ell.
\end{equation}

Key features: lazy evaluation, occupancy tracking with
\texttt{subtree\_occ} counters, and binary/mmap persistence with
incremental append.

\subsection{Dual-Channel Envelopes}

Collision queries use \texttt{derive\_merged\_link\_iaabb()} to take
element-wise tight bounds (hull) from both channels.
A source substitution matrix governs which cached sources can serve
which requests.

\subsection{Split Strategies}

Three strategies: \texttt{ROUND\_ROBIN} ($\mathrm{depth}\bmod D$),
\texttt{WIDEST\_FIRST}, and \texttt{BEST\_TIGHTEN} (default ---
multi-probe on top-$k$ widest dimensions, minimizing child-envelope
volume).

\subsection{Z4 Rotational Symmetry}

For robots with $90°$ symmetry at joint 0 (e.g., KUKA IIWA14), the
LECT computes envelopes only for the canonical sector; remaining
sectors via Z4 transform --- up to $4{\times}$ speedup.

\subsection{Incremental FK Optimization}

With IFK source and valid FK state, endpoint coordinates are
extracted directly from FK prefix matrices, bypassing
\texttt{compute\_endpoint\_iaabb()} entirely.
Unaffected links are copied via \texttt{memcpy}, reducing per-node
FK cost by 40--45\%.

\subsection{FFB: Find Free Box}

FFB lazily splits the LECT along a seed path until a collision-free,
unoccupied leaf is found.
\texttt{subtree\_occ} counters enable early termination on fully
occupied subtrees.

\subsection{Persistent Cache}

Cache path: \texttt{\textasciitilde/.sbf\_cache/<robot>\_<fp>.lect}
(FNV-1a 64-bit hash of DH parameters).
Incremental save appends only newly split nodes across runs.

\section{Offline Phase: Build Coverage}\label{sec:build}

\subsection{Coordinated Multi-Tree Growth}

When \texttt{connect\_mode = true}, forest growth and connectivity
are unified in a single coordinated loop.

\subsubsection{Root Selection}
One root box per seed configuration via FFB.
All trees share a single master LECT.

\subsubsection{Coordinated Growth Loop}
The master thread dispatches FFB tasks to a \texttt{ThreadPool};
each worker holds an independent LECT snapshot refreshed every
10~batches.

Key mechanisms:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Connection-driven scheduling}: with probability 0.7,
    select the tree in the smallest connected component (tracked via
    incremental UnionFind).
  \item \textbf{Adaptive sampling}: goal-biased (default 0.8),
    midpoint bridging (0.7 when stalled), or uniform fallback.
  \item \textbf{$K$-subsample}: for $>64$ boxes, sample 64 and pick
    nearest center (cache-friendly flat array).
  \item \textbf{Snap-to-face}: place seed at $\varepsilon$ outside the
    face with largest directional component.
  \item \textbf{Cross-tree adjacency}: on contact, unite trees in
    UnionFind.
\end{enumerate}

Growth continues until \texttt{max\_boxes}, timeout, or miss limit.
Connectivity triggers do \emph{not} halt growth.

\subsubsection{Inline Midpoint Bridging}
When stalled (500 boxes without component-count change), 70\% of
seeds switch to midpoint interpolation between frontier boxes of
disconnected components, with Gaussian perturbation
($\sigma = 0.02$).

\subsubsection{Promotion}
Merge sibling occupied LECT leaves whose parent envelope is
collision-free into one larger box.

\subsection{Multi-Level Coarsening}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Dimension-scan merge}: exact-contact pairs, safe by
    construction.
  \item \textbf{Greedy adjacency merge}: hull scoring
    ($V_\text{hull}/(V_a+V_b)$) + \texttt{hull\_region\_safe}
    verification (member-box pruning + AABB check + LECT traversal).
    Tarjan's algorithm protects articulation points.
  \item \textbf{Overlap filtering}: delete boxes fully contained in
    larger ones.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Adjacency Graph}

Two boxes are adjacent iff exactly one dimension is touching
($|w| \leq \text{tol}$) and all others overlap ($w > \text{tol}$):
$w_{ij}^{(d)} = \min(u_{i,d}, u_{j,d}) - \max(l_{i,d}, l_{j,d})$.

\section{Online Phase: Query Pipeline}\label{sec:query}

\subsection{Box Location and Connectivity}

Locate start/goal boxes (or nearest as fallback).
If in different components, attempt \texttt{bridge\_s\_t} (3\,s).

\subsection{Isolation Detection and Proxy Mechanism}

Tiered proxy search for isolated start/goal boxes:
Tier~1 (top~5, 200\,ms), Tier~2 (next~10, 500\,ms),
Tier~3 (remaining, 2000\,ms).
Total budget: 8000\,ms.

\subsection{A* Graph Search}

$f(n) = g(n) + h(n)$, $h(n) = \|c_n - q_g\|_2$; edge weight =
distance to shared-face center.
Fallback: direct RRT (3\,s, 200k iterations).

\subsection{P0: Multi-Trial RRT Competition}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Pre-competition greedy simplification.
  \item Up to 3 RRT trials with adaptive timeout.
  \item Competition: $\min(\text{chain}, \text{best\_rrt})$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{SBF vs.\ IRIS-NP: Computational Trade-offs}

IRIS-NP~\cite{werner2024fast} generates polytopic convex regions via
nonlinear programming, requiring 0.5--5\,s per region depending on DOF
and obstacle density.
A typical GCS graph for a 7-DOF scene requires 20--100 regions,
yielding decomposition times of 10--500\,s.
In contrast, SBF generates 4{,}650 boxes for the same 7-DOF combined
scene in 2.4\,s total.
While individual SBF boxes (axis-aligned) are less expressive than
IRIS polytopes, their larger count often improves coverage resolution.
A hybrid approach---using SBF boxes as seed regions for a small
number of IRIS-NP expansions---could capture the benefits of both.

\subsection{SBF as a GCS Front-End}

The SBF box forest maps directly to a GCS~\cite{marcucci2024motion}
graph: each box $\mathcal{B}_i$ becomes a vertex, box overlap defines
edges, and the convex relaxation yields globally optimal trajectories.
Corridor coarsening (\texttt{hull\_region\_safe} with depth-6
bisection) reduces the GCS vertex count from thousands to hundreds
when needed.
This positions SBF as a drop-in replacement for the IRIS-NP
decomposition step within the GCS pipeline, with the potential to
reduce total planning time substantially; in favorable settings this
could reduce runtime from minutes to seconds.

\subsection{Dual-Channel Necessity}

CH\_SAFE (IFK) guarantees conservative refutation---no false negatives
in collision checking.
CH\_UNSAFE (CritSample) provides tighter coverage, expanding the free
space discovered by the box forest.
The hull balances safety and tightness:
boxes are only accepted if CH\_SAFE confirms they are free,
but CH\_UNSAFE guides the splitting heuristic toward productive
regions.

\subsection{Pipeline Coupling and Negative Results}

Two optimizations were developed and reverted during development,
revealing implicit pipeline coupling.
P1~(2$\times$ collision resolution) introduced floating-point
non-determinism: boundary interpolation points exhibit
path-dependent rounding, causing occasional
\texttt{check\_segment} inconsistency.
Multi-layer fallbacks handle this effectively.
P3~(elastic band enhancement) improved local convergence but altered
intermediate topology, degrading downstream shortcut performance.
These negative results underscore the importance of end-to-end
ablation over isolated component benchmarks.

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Axis-aligned approximation.}
AABB regions are inherently less expressive than oriented bounding
boxes or general polytopes.
This is most pronounced in narrow passages oriented diagonally
relative to the joint axes, where many small boxes are required
for coverage.
The 4-obstacle scalability failure (\Cref{tab:scale}) reflects this
limitation.

\paragraph{Curse of dimensionality.}
While SBF handles 7-DOF effectively, the $2^D$ corner enumeration
and exponentially growing LECT splitting cost limit scalability
to higher DOF.
For 12+ DOF systems (dual-arm or mobile manipulation), hierarchical
decomposition strategies would be required.

\paragraph{No path optimality guarantee.}
The A* graph search produces the shortest graph path but not
the shortest continuous path.
Post-processing (elastic band + greedy shortcut) improves path quality
but provides no formal optimality bound.
Integration with GCS would address this limitation.

\subsection{Forest Reusability}

The LECT cache is bound to kinematics (DH parameters), not scene
geometry.
Changing obstacles requires only collision re-verification and
coarsening of the existing box forest.
The persistent binary/mmap cache
(\texttt{\textasciitilde/.sbf\_cache/}) enables sub-millisecond
LECT initialisation across planning runs and even across different
scenes with the same robot.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented SafeBoxForest, a two-phase motion planning framework
for high-dimensional serial manipulators that precomputes a dense
box cover of $\Cfree$ for fast online queries.
The key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Dual-channel LECT with $4{\times}3$ envelope pipeline, Z4
    acceleration, and incremental FK;
  \item Five-phase analytical solver with DH-chain direct extraction
    ($37\%$ speedup) and certified BnB verifier ($\geq 98\%$ fast path);
  \item Sparse Voxel BitMask with turbo scanline rasterization;
  \item Coordinated multi-tree growth with inline midpoint bridging;
  \item Multi-trial RRT + elastic band + multi-layer safety for online
    path quality;
  \item Modular 10-module / 42-header architecture;
  \item KUKA IIWA14 validation: 2.4\,s build, 100\% success,
    1.908--5.169\,rad paths, 0.024--0.468\,s queries;
  \item Franka Panda validation: 0.66\,s build, 100\% success,
    $3.6{\times}$ faster than IIWA14.
\end{enumerate}

The comprehensive ablation study (\Cref{tab:ablation}) demonstrates that
connect-stop (P4) is the dominant build optimization ($1.6{\times}$~speedup),
while BEST\_TIGHTEN split strategy provides the best overall
build-query tradeoff.
Per-scene experiments confirm sub-linear scaling with obstacle count
and consistent 100\% success rates across the tested geometries.
The persistent LECT cache enables sub-millisecond initialisation across
planning sessions.

\textbf{Future work.}
(1)~Integration with GCS~\cite{marcucci2024motion} for globally
optimal trajectories using the box forest as convex regions;
(2)~GPU-accelerated FK and collision checking for real-time
replanning;
(3)~Learned split and seed strategies via neural network surrogates;
(4)~Extension to branching kinematic chains and mobile manipulators
($>$7 DOF);
(5)~Incremental scene updates for dynamic environments;
(6)~Formal end-to-end comparison with IRIS-NP/GCS at matched
timescales;
(7)~AVX2/512 SIMD acceleration for BitBrick operations.
